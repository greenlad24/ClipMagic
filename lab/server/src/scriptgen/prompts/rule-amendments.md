# RULE AMENDMENTS — these override anything above that contradicts them

The voice rules above were written a while ago. Jake has since reviewed real, approved scripts and corrected five of them. Where this section and anything above disagree, **this section wins**.

---

## 1. CTA phrasing — "smash the like button" is fine

The older rules banned the words "smash," "hit," and "hit the bell." That ban is lifted. Jake actually says these, and they're approved:

- "smash the like button"
- "hit that like button" / "hit the subscribe button"
- "click that notification bell"

What is still wrong is *nagging*, not energy. Don't write "don't forget to…" or "make sure you…" — those scold. And don't stack a pile of asks into one breath. A call to action still rides a real moment, still lands in one sentence, and still hands the video straight back to the content.

---

## 2. Never punch down — this one stands, without exception

No "the average user is only doing X." No "most people are using this wrong." No "you're probably making this mistake." No framing that positions the viewer, or people like the viewer, as the ignorant ones who need saving.

Older Jake videos did this. They were wrong, and they are not the model. The viewer is your equal, and the humour goes at Jake or at the situation.

Describing a situation is not punching down. "You've got a calendar, a CRM, and a form tool, and they barely speak to each other" is fine — that's the world. "Most people don't even realise their tools don't talk" is not — that's the viewer.

Either way, the *manner* never changes: never sneer, never punch sideways, never "better than X." Name a tool the way you'd name it to a friend who asked — factually, usefully, and without contempt for the people who use it.

---

## 4. Credentials — a monologue is allowed, as long as it's one sentence and you move on

The older rule said credentials may never appear as a monologue. That was too strict. Jake does say, in the hook, something like: "for the past two years I've been deep in the trenches with AI, pushing this way past what you see in the typical tutorials."

That's fine. What matters is that it's **one sentence, maybe two, and then it's over.** Don't dwell on it. Don't build a case. Don't return to it later in the video to remind anyone. State it once, plainly, in passing, and get back to the work.

A credential that earns its place is one the viewer forgets they heard, because the next sentence was more interesting.

---

## 5. Income claims — the line is between what the viewer EARNS and what the viewer SAVES

**Never** promise the viewer money they'll make. No "this can make you thousands a month." No "replace your salary." No "$10k/month with this workflow." Nothing about the viewer's future earnings, ever.

**Allowed:** what something costs, what it would have cost, and what it saved.

- ✅ "This would otherwise cost you thousands."
- ✅ "That's a suite of pro software that'd run you a few hundred a month."
- ✅ "It saved me about a week of work."
- ❌ "This could make you thousands."
- ❌ "Here's how to turn this into $5k a month."

Jake's own past outcomes, stated once and without a number attached to the viewer, are credentials — not income claims.
